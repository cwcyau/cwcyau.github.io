The Value of Health Data

Jan 4, 2026 17 min read Health Data

This blog is part of a short series of articles I will be writing to welcome the start of 2026. All opinions expressed are my own and do not represent the views of any individuals or organisations mentioned in the articles. Further, I am a UK-based academic scientist and these articles reflect the UK context.

What is the value of health data?

In the last two posts, I discussed doctoral education and the development of research programmes and in particular the EIT Fundamentals of AI Centre for Doctoral Training at Oxford. A particular area of interest for the CDT is health, medicine and biology aligning to one of the core themes of EIT and the strengths of the university in this area.

The availability and use of health data is clearly critical for this area and, for a number of years in the UK, there has been sustained investment in the idea that health data is a valuable commodity for research. High visibility initiatives such as the UK Biobank, Genomics England and Our Future Health position the UK as a global leader in population-level health data research resourcing and this is on top of the myriad of many additional public and charity funded projects that support data in many different disease areas and population groups.

As a consequence, health data is frequently positioned as a national asset: something that can drive scientific discovery, improve public health, increase the efficiency of the UK National Health Service (NHS), and support economic growth through innovation. It is cited as a critical ingredient for advances in areas such as drug discovery, precision medicine, disease prevention, and health system planning. This narrative has been reinforced by significant political attention, major funding initiatives, and the creation of new organisations, infrastructures, and strategies dedicated to unlocking the potential of health data.

At times, this enthusiasm has bordered on inevitability: that once health data is made available at scale, benefits will naturally follow.

But how much of this is grounded in reality, and how much is hype?

In this article, I want to step back from policy slogans and investment headlines and give my own perspective on what the value of health data actually is, where it genuinely lies, and where expectations may need to be recalibrated.

I focus specifically on the value of health data for generating insight into disease aetiology and for informing patient management or treatment research and development. I do not consider the use of health data for software product development aimed at operational optimisation (such as waiting-time management or bed prioritisation), nor for assistive tools (for example, speech-to-text transcription).

Nor is this an article about under-resourcing within the UK health system or the limitations of existing health IT infrastructure, important though those issues undoubtedly are.

What is health data?

Health data broadly refers to any data that reveals information about the health state of individuals or populations.

This includes what most people intuitively think of as health data: clinical records generated by GPs, hospitals, and other healthcare providers. Diagnoses, prescriptions, laboratory results, imaging, procedures, and outcomes all fall into this category. These records are typically created as part of routine care rather than research, which has important implications for their structure and quality.

However, health data extends well beyond formal medical records. Many other forms of personal and administrative data can indirectly reveal information about health. Where you live, how much you earn, what you spend money on, how you travel, your employment history, and your interactions with social services can all provide insights into health status, risk factors, and outcomes. Increasingly, data from wearable devices, smartphones, and other consumer technologies also contribute to this broader health data ecosystem.

A critical but often overlooked point is that data must be electronically stored and accessible in order to be usable. Historically, this has not always been the case. Large volumes of health information were recorded on paper, stored in fragmented systems, or locked into IT infrastructures that could not communicate with one another. Even today, interoperability remains a major challenge, limiting the extent to which data can be linked, analysed, and reused.

As a result, the existence of health data does not guarantee its availability for research and development, nor its suitability for answering meaningful questions and product development.

So far we have only discussed health data in the context of routinely collected data as part of healthcare service delivery or other general life activities. This means data that is acquired is often primarily to support some other function (eg prescription data enables pharmacists to reclaim money, retailer loyalty cards are used to maintain customer relationships, etc). However, some definitions of health data can also encompass information gathered from an experiments designed to test a single or small set of hypotheses. This can include purely laboratory-based experiments or real-world experiments (eg a clinical trial). These data often have the sole purpose of answering the experimental question but have limited use otherwise. I referred to these broadly as experimental data to distinguish them from routinely collected data. In this article, I will implicitly assume a focus on routinely collected data rather than experimental data.

The promise of health data

The case for investing in health data is, on the surface, compelling.

From a research perspective, large-scale health data can enable the study of disease patterns that would be invisible in small cohorts. It can support earlier detection of disease, more accurate risk stratification, and a better understanding of how treatments work in real-world settings rather than controlled clinical trials. For drug discovery, access to longitudinal health records linked to genetics and outcomes promises to reduce failure rates and identify new therapeutic targets.

From a public health standpoint, health data can inform policy decisions, support surveillance of emerging threats, and help evaluate the impact of interventions. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the importance of timely, high-quality data became starkly apparent, reinforcing arguments for better data infrastructure and analytic capacity.

Within the NHS, health data is often framed as a tool for improving efficiency: reducing duplication, optimising pathways, identifying unwarranted variation, and supporting more proactive and preventative models of care.

In the commercial sector, access to health data can enable the development of new health technologies which the translation of research concepts into practical health solutions.

Taken together, these opportunities help explain why health data has attracted such political and financial support. Few other resources appear to offer the possibility of simultaneously improving health outcomes, strengthening public services, and driving economic return.

The realities of health data

Much of the health data available for research is not collected with research in mind. Routinely collected data primarily exists to support the delivery, administration, and financing of healthcare. It serves business, operational, and regulatory purposes first, with research and health improvement often being secondary or unintended uses.

This misalignment matters. Research questions are rarely shaped around what data happens to exist; rather, data availability constrains what questions can realistically be asked. As a result, there is often a degree of fortune involved in whether a routinely collected health dataset is relevant to a particular research question or use case. It is important to accept — uncomfortably, at times — that some datasets are simply not useful for the questions we would most like to answer.

Health data is typically generated when an action is taken: when a patient presents, when a test is ordered, when a diagnosis is made, or when a treatment is delivered. If nothing happens, nothing is recorded. This means that the periods that are often of greatest interest for prevention and early intervention — pre-diagnosis, early disease onset, or subtle changes in health status — are frequently missing or poorly captured. No data means no insight, no matter how sophisticated the analytic methods applied downstream.

This presents a particular challenge for research aimed at prevention or high-efficiency treatment strategies, which depend on understanding disease processes early. In the absence of data from these early stages, researchers are often forced to assume that relevant signals persist into later stages of disease. Sometimes they do. Often they do not. This assumption can introduce bias and limit the extent to which findings translate into effective early interventions.

Routine healthcare data also reflects what actually happened within a given system, under prevailing clinical guidelines, resource constraints, and institutional norms. It captures the enactment of medical decisions, not the full space of possible decisions. Yet many research questions — particularly those concerned with optimisation, fairness, or alternative pathways — implicitly ask counterfactual questions: what might have happened if something else had been done?

Where diagnosis and treatment pathways are well established, examples of alternative approaches may be rare or entirely absent from the data. This limits the ability to evaluate different strategies, compare outcomes, or learn from variation. The data tells us what was done, not necessarily what could have been done, and the distinction is crucial.

As a consequence, the context in which health data is generated can be as important as the raw values it contains. Understanding why data exists, what incentives shaped its collection, what is systematically missing, and whose perspectives are represented — or excluded — is essential for interpreting results responsibly. Without this contextual understanding, there is a risk of over-interpreting findings, overstating confidence, or drawing conclusions that the data was never capable of supporting.

Recognising these realities does not diminish the value of health data. But it does require a more mature and honest conversation about what health data can and cannot do, and where investment in data infrastructure needs to be complemented by investment in domain knowledge, methodological rigour, and critical interpretation.

The lure of AI

It is understandable that the health sector has looked to the successes of AI in other domains and sought to replicate them. In areas such as online retail, streaming platforms, and digital media, the combination of large datasets and machine learning has enabled new ways to understand user behaviour, personalise experiences, and generate substantial economic value. From the outside, it can appear natural to apply the same logic to health data.

Recommendation systems that suggest songs on Spotify, films on Netflix, or short-form videos on YouTube are often cited as examples of what is possible when large-scale data and AI are combined effectively. However, these tasks are, in important respects, far less complex than many problems in healthcare. If a recommendation system performs poorly, the consequences are usually minor: a skipped track or a disengaged user. In contrast, many applications of AI in health are mission - or safety-critical, where errors can have serious consequences for individuals and health systems.

There are also fundamental differences in how data is collected. In consumer-facing domains, data is often gathered both actively (for example, through account activity) and passively (such as through web tracking or usage logs), resulting in rich, continuous behavioural profiles. In healthcare, by contrast, data collection is necessarily constrained by privacy, consent, and ethical considerations. This is entirely appropriate, but it means that health records typically contain focused, episodic information rather than a comprehensive picture of an individual’s life and context.

As a result, health data often lacks the surrounding contextual information that helps make sense of observations. Medical events are recorded, but the circumstances leading up to them may not be. This makes direct transfer of AI approaches from consumer domains to healthcare problematic. The risk is not that AI has no role to play in health, but that expectations shaped elsewhere lead to overconfidence about what health data can realistically support.

Lure of causality

Closely related to the enthusiasm for AI is the appeal of causal inference. Causal language is frequently used when discussing health data, particularly in the context of uncovering disease aetiology, identifying risk factors, or informing intervention strategies.

In principle, causal inference provides a powerful framework for reasoning about why things happen, not just what happens. In practice, however, identifying causal relationships from observational health data alone is extremely challenging. Any serious engagement with causal inference quickly reveals the strength of the assumptions required to support causal claims.

In the absence of randomisation or natural experiments, causal identifiability often depends on assumptions about unmeasured confounding, measurement error, selection bias, and the structure of the underlying system. These assumptions are rarely fully testable using the data at hand. As a result, causal conclusions drawn from observational health data should be treated with appropriate caution.

It is important to be clear that the use of AI does not resolve these challenges. Machine learning can improve prediction, pattern recognition, and scalability, but it does not remove the fundamental limits imposed by the data-generating process. AI is not a magic wand that turns observational data into causal truth. Without careful study design, domain knowledge, and explicit assumptions, causal claims remain fragile - no matter how sophisticated the models involved.

(Epi)c findings

Large-scale health datasets have long been appealing to epidemiologists, and for good reason. At population scale, these data enable the identification of associations between exposures, behaviours, and outcomes that would be invisible in smaller studies. They allow researchers to characterise trends, stratify risk, and generate hypotheses at relatively low marginal cost.

Findings from such analyses are typically expressed in terms of average effects and broad population-level patterns. This is entirely appropriate for many purposes, including public health surveillance, policy evaluation, and prioritisation of further research. Population-level insight is one of the genuine strengths of routinely collected health data.

However, many of the applications that attract attention — particularly in clinical settings — require something different. They require individual-level predictions that are sufficiently accurate, specific, and robust to inform decisions about individual patients. The gap between population-level association and individual-level utility is often underestimated.

An effect that is statistically significant at the population level may still be too weak, too variable, or too context-dependent to be useful for individual decision-making. Signals that are meaningful in aggregate can wash out when applied to individuals, particularly in heterogeneous populations. As a result, health data that is valuable for epidemiological discovery may not translate into actionable tools for patient-level care.

This distinction matters because much of the excitement around health data and AI implicitly assumes that insights derived from large datasets can be straightforwardly deployed at the bedside. In reality, doing so requires careful validation, clear articulation of intended use, and an understanding of the trade-offs between sensitivity, specificity, and uncertainty.

Health data is exceptionally powerful for describing patterns and generating insight at scale. Turning those insights into reliable, individual-level interventions is a much harder problem—one that cannot be solved by more data or more complex models alone.

Digital Twins

I have seen the term “digital twins” used extensively in recent years, particularly in discussions about the future of health data and personalised medicine. Broadly, a digital twin refers to a computational model that seeks to simulate the behaviour of a real-world system - such as a cell, an organ, or an entire person. The underlying idea is that we can perturb or alter the digital twin in-silico to explore how its properties change, without having to perform the corresponding intervention in the real world, where doing so might be dangerous, unethical, or prohibitively expensive in terms of time and cost.

The idea of using individual-level health data to build patient-specific digital twins is, on the surface, extremely attractive. If I had a faithful digital twin of a person, I could simulate what might happen if I prescribed a different drug, altered a treatment pathway, or introduced a preventative intervention. In principle, this could allow us to explore long-term outcomes without waiting years to observe them, and to move beyond reliance on population-average effects from clinical trials that can obscure meaningful heterogeneity in patient response.

However, the practical reality is considerably more complicated.

Health data is almost always collected for clinical reasons, not for the purpose of building complete models of individuals. As a result, it provides a highly selective and biased view of a person’s life course. We tend to record data when something happens — when someone becomes unwell, seeks care, receives a diagnosis, or undergoes treatment. We rarely collect rich, continuous measurements during periods of good health, and we almost never observe what would have happened had no intervention taken place.

This creates a fundamental problem for digital twin modelling. The data required to support accurate simulation of counterfactual scenarios—what if we did something different, or nothing at all — is precisely the data that is missing. As a consequence, current approaches to digital twins in healthcare must rely on strong modelling assumptions about unobserved states, disease progression, and treatment effects. These assumptions are often informed by population-level data, expert judgement, or simplified mechanistic models, rather than direct observation.

None of this makes digital twins uninteresting or without value. They can be powerful tools for hypothesis generation, for integrating disparate sources of knowledge, and for exploring plausible scenarios under clearly stated assumptions. But it does mean that their outputs should be interpreted as conditional and exploratory rather than predictive in any definitive sense.

In this respect, digital twins are a useful illustration of a broader theme in health data research. As models become more sophisticated and individualised, the limitations of the underlying data do not disappear—they become more consequential. The danger is not in building digital twins, but in forgetting how much of their behaviour is driven by assumptions rather than evidence.

Used carefully, digital twins may help us reason more explicitly about uncertainty, mechanisms, and heterogeneity. Used incautiously, they risk creating an illusion of individual-level precision that the data simply cannot support. As with many applications of health data and AI, their value lies not in replacing judgement, but in informing it—provided we remain honest about what is known, what is assumed, and what remains fundamentally unknowable.

Effective health data research

I have only actively worked in the health data domain for a few years, but that has been long enough to make me acutely conscious of the risks of exaggerated claims.

In recent years, health data research — particularly where it intersects with AI — has (in my opinion) had a tendency to over-promise. Results are often framed in ways that suggest imminent clinical impact, causal insight, or transformative change, even when the underlying evidence is preliminary, context-dependent, or heavily qualified. This is not unique to health data, but the stakes in this domain make the consequences more serious.

Part of the problem is structural. Incentives in academia, industry, and funding all reward novelty, scale, and apparent impact. Claims that are careful, conditional, and honest about uncertainty rarely travel as far as those that are bold and definitive. Over time, this can create a culture in which optimism shades into hype, and where limitations are treated as inconveniences rather than central features of the problem.

This article is not intended to devalue the utility of health data. On the contrary, health data is extraordinarily valuable in the right context. It is indispensable for population health analysis, hypothesis generation, surveillance, and for understanding how health systems behave in practice. It can provide genuine insight into disease processes and inform research and development when used appropriately.

But the value of health data is not maximised by overstating what it can do. If anything, it is diminished by exaggerated or unverifiable claims that cannot withstand scrutiny or fail to translate into real-world benefit. Over time, this erodes trust — among clinicians, policymakers, patients, and the public — and makes it harder to sustain the very investments on which the field depends.

Effective health data research requires restraint as much as ambition. It requires clarity about what questions can reasonably be answered, what assumptions are being made, and where uncertainty remains. It also requires resisting the temptation to imply causality where only association exists, or individual-level utility where only population-level insight has been demonstrated.

Personally, the research insights I trust most are those where authors are explicit not only about what is exciting, but also about the caveats, assumptions, and limitations that shape their conclusions. Truly holistic answers are rare in research; more often, progress involves exercising best judgement based on an interpretation of imperfect evidence — and that is entirely acceptable when the limitations are clearly acknowledged.

In fact, without recognising and engaging seriously with those limitations, we may miss some of the most important and impactful fundamental research opportunities of all.

Ultimately, the long-term value of health data will depend less on how much of it we collect, and more on how carefully, honestly, and responsibly we interpret it. That, in my view, is the standard we should be holding ourselves to as the field continues to mature.
